## What does it mean for a function to be continuous?

Informally, a function is **continuous** at a point if you can draw its graph through that point without lifting your pen. Formally, continuity requires three conditions to hold simultaneously.

**A function $f$ is continuous at $x = a$ if and only if:**

1. $f(a)$ is defined
2. $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists
3. $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$

All three must hold. Failing any one produces a **discontinuity**.

---

## Types of discontinuity

### Removable discontinuity

The limit exists, but either $f(a)$ is undefined or $f(a) \neq \lim_{x \to a} f(x)$.

**Example:**
$$f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} \quad \text{at } x = 1$$

The limit is 2, but $f(1)$ is undefined. Defining $f(1) = 2$ *removes* the discontinuity.

### Jump discontinuity

The one-sided limits both exist but are not equal:

$$\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \neq \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)$$

Cannot be removed by redefining a single point.

### Infinite discontinuity

The function grows without bound near $a$: $f(x) \to \pm \infty$ as $x \to a$.

**Example:** $f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}$ at $x = 0$.

---

## Continuity on an interval

- **Open interval $(a, b)$:** $f$ is continuous at every point in $(a, b)$.
- **Closed interval $[a, b]$:** $f$ is continuous on $(a, b)$, right-continuous at $a$, and left-continuous at $b$.

### Continuous functions — a catalogue

The following are continuous on their natural domains:

- Polynomial functions: $p(x) = a_n x^n + \cdots + a_0$
- Rational functions: $\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}$ where $q(x) \neq 0$
- Root functions: $\sqrt[n]{x}$ (with domain restrictions for even $n$)
- Trigonometric functions: $\sin x$, $\cos x$, and their ratios where defined
- Exponential and logarithmic functions

---

## Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT)

The IVT is one of the most powerful consequences of continuity.

> **Theorem.** If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and $N$ is any value strictly between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, then there exists at least one $c \in (a, b)$ such that $f(c) = N$.

**Intuition:** a continuous function cannot jump over a value — it must pass through every intermediate value.

**Classic application — existence of roots:**

Show that $f(x) = x^3 - x - 1$ has a root in $(1, 2)$.

- $f(1) = 1 - 1 - 1 = -1 < 0$
- $f(2) = 8 - 2 - 1 = 5 > 0$

Since $f$ is a polynomial (continuous everywhere) and $f$ changes sign on $[1, 2]$, by the IVT there exists $c \in (1, 2)$ with $f(c) = 0$.

> **Note:** the IVT guarantees *existence* — it does not find the root's exact location.
