## What is a limit?

Calculus is fundamentally the study of *change* — and limits are the language that makes that study precise.

Informally, the **limit** of a function $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches a value $a$ is the value that $f(x)$ gets arbitrarily close to, as $x$ gets closer and closer to $a$ — without ever reaching $a$.

We write this as:

$$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$$

This reads: *"the limit of f of x, as x approaches a, equals L."*

---

## A concrete example

Consider the function:

$$f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}$$

This function is **undefined at $x = 1$** (division by zero). Yet we can ask: what value does $f(x)$ approach as $x$ gets close to 1?

Factor the numerator:

$$f(x) = \frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1} = x + 1 \quad \text{for } x \neq 1$$

As $x \to 1$, we get $f(x) \to 2$. So:

$$\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} = 2$$

> **Key insight:** the limit does not care what happens *at* $x = 1$ — only what happens *near* it.

---

## One-sided limits

Sometimes a function approaches different values from the left and from the right.

- **Left-hand limit:** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x)$ — as $x$ approaches $a$ from values *less than* $a$
- **Right-hand limit:** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)$ — as $x$ approaches $a$ from values *greater than* $a$

The two-sided limit $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists **if and only if** both one-sided limits exist and are equal:

$$\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L$$

---

## When limits do not exist

A limit fails to exist in three typical situations:

1. **Jump discontinuity** — left and right limits exist but differ
2. **Infinite behaviour** — $f(x) \to \pm\infty$
3. **Oscillation** — $f(x)$ oscillates without settling (e.g. $\sin(1/x)$ near 0)
